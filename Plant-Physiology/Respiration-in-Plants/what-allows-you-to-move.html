What allows you to move?

(A) Your mitochondria use respiration to create lipids to make you move

(B) Your mitochondria use respiration to create enzymes to make you move

(C) Your mitochondria use respiration to create proteins to make you move

(D) ** Your mitochondria use respiration to create ATP to make you move

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The electrochemical proton gradient across the inner mitochondrial membrane is used to drive ATP synthesis in the critical process of oxidative phosphorylation (Figure 14-14). This is made possible by the membrane-bound enzyme ATP synthase, mentioned previously.

Concept note-2: -The mitochondria are small organelles floating freely throughout the cell. Mitochondria utilize oxygen to produce ATP in cellular respiration. These membrane-bound cell organelles synthesize the energy required for cellular activities or biochemical reactions.

Concept note-3: -Most of the adenosine triphosphate (ATP) synthesized during glucose metabolism is produced in the mitochondria through oxidative phosphorylation. This is a complex reaction powered by the proton gradient across the mitochondrial inner membrane, which is generated by mitochondrial respiration.

Concept note-4: -Protons flow down their concentration gradient into the matrix through the membrane protein ATP synthase, causing it to spin (like a water wheel) and catalyze conversion of ADP to ATP.

Concept note-5: -The reactants of cellular respiration vary at each stage, but initially, it requires an input of glucose, ATP, and NAD+. NAD+, a nicotinamide derived from vitamin B3, is a universal electron acceptor that is crucial in the process of cellular respiration.
