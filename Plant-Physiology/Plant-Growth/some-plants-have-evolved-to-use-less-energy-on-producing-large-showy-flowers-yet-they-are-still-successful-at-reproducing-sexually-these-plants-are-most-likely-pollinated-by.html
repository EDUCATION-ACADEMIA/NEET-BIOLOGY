PLANT GROWTH

[SOURCES]
Some plants have evolved to use less energy on producing large showy flowers, yet they are still successful at reproducing sexually. These plants are most likely pollinated by

(A) animals that can see different wavelengths of light.

(B) small insects that have a very keen sense of smell.

(C) ** the wind dispersing their pollen large distances.

(D) self-pollination methods.

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The more exclusive the relationship between a plant species and its pollinator, the better for the plant; If a pollinator visits only one species of flower, that flower is assured that its pollen will find its way to another of its kind.

Concept note-2: -Plants and their pollinators form a mutualistic relationship, a relationship in which each benefits from the other. In the plant-pollinator relationship, the pollinator benefits by feeding on food rewards provided by the flower, primarily nectar and pollen.

Concept note-3: -Unlike the typical insect-pollinated flowers, flowers adapted to pollination by wind do not produce nectar or scent. In wind-pollinated species, the microsporangia hang out of the flower, and, as the wind blows, the lightweight pollen is carried with it.

Concept note-4: -Angiosperms evolved during the late Cretaceous Period, about 125-100 million years ago. Angiosperms have developed flowers and fruit as ways to attract pollinators and protect their seeds, respectively. Flowers have a wide array of colors, shapes, and smells, all of which are for the purpose of attracting pollinators.
